<p>Robin is wondering whether the <a href="https://robinrendle.com/notes/So-Many-Websites/">decline of search</a> might actually lead to a better and smaller web:</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-style-plain is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow is-style-plain--3">
<p>But perhaps the death of search is&nbsp;<em>good</em>&nbsp;for the future of the web. Perhaps websites can be free of dumb rankings and junky ads that are designed to make fractions of a penny at a time. Perhaps the web needs to be released from the burden of this business model. Perhaps mass readership isnâ€™t possible for the vast majority of websites and was never really sustainable in the first place.</p>
</blockquote>



<p>The analogy with books is exactly relevant.  There are so, so many books and most don&#8217;t have mass readership.  And books are thriving more than ever in the digital age.</p>



<p>I <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/not-on-google/" data-type="post" data-id="515">don&#8217;t allow my personal websites</a> to be indexed by search engines.  I did at first, and used to spend time in the google search console trying to figure out why they refused to index one third of the posts. </p>



<p>In the end, I thought to myself &#8220;well, if you&#8217;re not going to index them all, then you&#8217;re not going to index any&#8221; and denied access to all in my robots.txt.</p>



<p>Now I actively relish being stumbled upon rather than discovered.  I also have a steady trickle of lovely <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/guest-book/" data-type="post" data-id="187">comments in my guest book</a>, which gives me a much bigger dopamine hit than any SEO traffic dashboard ever could.</p>



<p>My little corner of the web is doing just fine without search.</p>
