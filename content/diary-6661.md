<p>Snow covered ground and icy winds were not on my bingo card this morning as we gathered at one of our most popular habitat management sites.  We delayed the start until 10am to be sure it wouldn&#8217;t get worse, and help travel times.</p>



<p>I was here last in <a href="https://diary.uncountable.uk/2025/08/harvesting-on-the-hill/" data-type="post" data-id="6375">the summer for seed harvesting</a>, and today couldn&#8217;t be more different.  My feet were blocks of ice even before we started.  The main tasks today were removing a tree plantation to open up more grassland and cut tracks in the main grassland where the Duke of Burgundy butterfly can be found.</p>

<p>By tea break, Tom had to stop cutting as Anna had to take the truck away from site.  However, it turned out there was more than enough to rake for the rest of the day.  It&#8217;s a very different profile to brush cutting where there might be two of us for most of the day.  People are also reluctant to rake next to the noisy cutter, so it&#8217;s much better to get everything cut in the first couple of hours and then spend the rest of the session in a proper rake off.</p>



<p>The tracks in the grassland follow a path we&#8217;ve established in previous years where we are trying to create different sward heights that the Dukes find desirable in a habitat.  There was an enormous amount of grass that the power scythe had removed &#8211; most patches required a second, or even third rake to completely remove the clippings.</p>



<p>By 2.30pm, we were starting to flag and had to carry the tools back down the hill back to warm cars.</p>
