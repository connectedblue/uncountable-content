<p>There&#8217;s major work to do at the back of the house and half the ground floor.  Planning permission has been granted, technical drawings completed and budget is in place.</p>



<p>All I need to do now is find someone to do it.  </p>



<p>Having been burned on timescales in three projects before, I really want to make sure this time that the builder sticks with it until the very end, and doesn&#8217;t mysteriously vanish for weeks at a time.</p>



<p>The workmanship has always been first class &#8211; the days of the dodgy builder are by and large gone, thanks to building regulations and better training.  However, communication still leaves a lot to be desired.  When you are living in the property being renovated, it&#8217;s a big deal having whole weeks of no progress and not knowing when you&#8217;ll see them again.</p>



<p>So this time my approach is going to be different.  For starters, I&#8217;ve written a 7 page Request For Quotation that details every aspect of requirement, along with the drawings.  I&#8217;m going to send it to builders who meet the following criteria:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Member of the Federation of Master Builders.  </li>



<li>Issue contracts with full scope and dispute resolution process with escalation to FMB.  This protects both of us.</li>



<li>Are willing to quote against my RFQ.  It&#8217;s an investment of their time to produce a detailed quotation which they may not win, but I prefer a builder who treats this as a cost of business.</li>



<li>Have a website and email address.  I want to read a little about them, and also contact them by writing.  Builders who only have a mobile number and check their outlook email once a quarter are out.</li>



<li>Most recent accounts at companies house show positive net assets and cash balance of at least £10,000.  Good financial management gives me confidence.</li>



<li>Debtors are greater than creditors due within a year.  So broadly speaking they have more coming in that going out.</li>
</ul>



<p>It&#8217;s been quite tricky to find people to even send a quote to.  There&#8217;s no shortage of candidates on the FMB approved list in my area &#8211; pages and pages of results.</p>



<p>The first test that most fail is  having positive net assets.  Some whose website look really promising turned out to have accumulated net losses in the hundreds of thousands.  There might be a really good reason for this, but assuming they are not fraudulently filing, accounts don&#8217;t lie.  </p>



<p>My fear is that they will feel the need to go off and do a new job to get money to pay for people and materials on mine.  </p>



<p>Some had net positive assets, but significantly more creditors than debtors.  So overall they might be in a good financial position, but cash flow could be an issue.  Again, I don&#8217;t want to be the customer who gets to find that out the hard way.</p>



<p>I managed to find six who met the criteria, and two are providing quotations.  I&#8217;m looking for one more and then hopefully I&#8217;ll have a good basis to move onto the actual start of construction later in the year.</p>
