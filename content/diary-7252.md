<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It was the annual shindig at the Royal Ag for the cotswold wardens, and <a href="https://www.cotswolds-nl.org.uk/looking-after/our-grasslands-projects/glorious-cotswolds-grasslands/">Glorious Grasslands</a> volunteers are invited along.  I picked up Niamh early and we were parked up by 9.30am.  </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">We kicked off with some stats and achievements over the last year from the CEO and head warden and then went to the first speaker, Arthur Price. It was obvious from the start that he&#8217;s a bit of a force of nature. As well as being a farmer, he&#8217;s a significant expert on Cotswold stone and quarring.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">He gave us a really thorough talk on the history of quarrying around the Cotswolds through the ages.  It was packed with facts &#8211; too many to remember, but the two takeaways for me were the different types of stone from different depths and also that Nailsworth is one of the most significant quarry areas in the Cotswolds &#8211; mostly underground.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">After the break, we had a Nature Recovery talk from Simon Smith who went through the basics and urgency of what needs to be done across the Cotswolds.  It seems as if there is now a comprehensive plan across all six counties that make up the area.  It just needs the incentives and action on the ground &#8211; mostly to be delivered by farmers with the correct support in place.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There was a pretty interesting Q&amp;A, followed by the most delicious food in the Refectory &#8211; Veggie Lasagne with potatoes, veg and white wine sauce followed by Eton Mess.</p>

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Then it was over to Frocester Court for a tour of the farm buildings owned by Arthur Price.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I&#8217;ve driven past this farm literally hundreds of times and had no idea what was going on behind that hedge.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">One of the most interesting things about this farm is that it is the only place in the UK which has documented evidence of continuous farming for the last 5,000 years.</p>

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Arthur&#8217;s father had undertaken extensive archeological works on the farm in the 1950s, initally uncovering a Roman Villa, but on further inspection the layers went deeper through the bronze age and neolithic.  The centerpiece of the buildings was a 13th century estate barn, which was enormous and very grand.  The roof was from the 16th century as the original had burned down, and there was extensive evidence of where part of the barn was used for human habitation during periods of lean years in farming.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">We toured all the buildings and workshops and were bombarded with information about geology, stone types and building history.  It was utterly fascinating.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Overall, this was an excellent conference on all fronts.</p>
