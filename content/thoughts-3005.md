<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I set up a password manager around 2010, and it remains the single best digital life move I&#8217;ve made.  Only one password to remember and every online account ever opened is recorded there &#8211; each with a long and unique password.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Apart from the obvious security benefits, one advantage of storing everything centrally is a single registry of all your online accounts.  Would you still be able to remember that florist you signed up with to send flowers one time in 2014 otherwise?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A lot of accounts naturally close down when you finish with the service &#8211; particularly financial ones. So there&#8217;s an ever growing list in the password manager bin of credentials that no longer work.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">However, there are still probably around 150+ active accounts created over the last 16 years. Even many where I have literally no idea why it&#8217;s there. Something called applitools created 4 years ago? A fancy dress shop all the way back in 2012??</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">As part of shrinking my digital surface area, I am going through them all slowly and removing accounts which I no longer use, need or want.  It&#8217;s a bit of a chore, but oddly satisfying when I can move another entry to the password manager bin.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Some accounts have an account deletion button, which either does it straight away or after 24 hours or so.  Those are very simple.  Once confirmed, I verify that the login no longer works and purge it from my life.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But a surprising number have no automated process.  With the help of Gemini, I have to send a message to support requesting deletion under GDPR.  These then take longer to fulfil.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">So far I&#8217;d say around 20 accounts have either been deleted entirely, or are in the process of being so.  I&#8217;ll keep chipping away at the list &#8211; there&#8217;s probably another 50 that can go.  </p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Although the risk of keeping any single dormant account alive is pretty minimal, if you have dozens of them your collective surface area for attacks, data breaches or fraud increases quite a bit.  So I feel better having conducted this piece of digital admin.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I dread to think how people without a password manager would go about such a task.  I suppose the answer is that you just have to hope that everything will be OK.</p>
