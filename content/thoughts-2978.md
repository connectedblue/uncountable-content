<p>Although I&#8217;ve been retired for several years, I don&#8217;t have a monthly income land in my current account automatically. I keep a cash buffer to cover <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/discretionary-vs-non-discretionary-spend/" data-type="post" data-id="512">four years of non-discretionary expenses</a> which lives across both my SIPP and normal taxable savings accounts.</p>



<p>For day to day expenses, I&#8217;m just transferring what&#8217;s needed from the savings into current account.  I keep track of the whole thing <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/plan-to-be-wealthy/" data-type="post" data-id="1844">on a quarterly basis</a> by reviewing current valuations and trailing 12 month non-discretionary spend.  This helps to decide appropriate points to top up the cash buffer.</p>



<p>More recently I&#8217;ve been creating part of the <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/cashing-out-for-the-future/" data-type="post" data-id="2689">cash buffer inside the SIPP itself</a>.  This is done by selling down index funds and buying MMF which are essentially like a safe savings rate.  Because the buffer is four years in size, and I make annual withdrawals  from the SIPP, it doesn&#8217;t all need to live in taxable cash accounts.  I may as well earn a little bit of tax free interest inside the SIPP.  It also gives the possibility of perhaps re-investing it if my plans changed (for example, receiving a large cash sum from somewhere). </p>



<p>It&#8217;s only when you withdraw from a SIPP are you subject to tax and other rules.  Up until then you are free to create whatever investment profile you choose.</p>



<p>So, back to this year&#8217;s tax year plan &#8230;</p>



<p>One of the major changes I made recently was to slowly drain my SIPP and recycle it into my ISA over the next 20 years.  The reasons for doing this are <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/draining-and-recycling-the-sipp/" data-type="post" data-id="2217">quite complicated and described here</a>, but in essence I&#8217;m making good use of my annual 20% tax allowance which expires every year if not used.</p>



<p>As soon as April 6 ticks over, I write down a detailed set of steps to execute over the next few weeks.   Each one is not complicated, but they have to be done in sequence and over a long period, so writing the exact amounts that flow where, when and why is essential to keep track.</p>



<p>My steps this year were:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Move residual amounts in general investing accounts into ISA using Bed &amp; ISA.  This is for both mine and my wife&#8217;s portfolio.  GIA is now zero for the first time in decades which simplifies aspects of tax returns and also means all invested assets are under tax efficient wrappers.</li>



<li>Sell down the MMF into cash balance needed for the withdrawals.  No investments needed to be sold, so things like Iran war and all the other emotional market stuff didn&#8217;t even need to be considered.</li>



<li>Crystallise a large amount.  Usually I do UFPLS as simpler, one shot approach to this annual withdrawal, but this year I need a larger amount of tax free cash to pay for <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/finding-a-builder/" data-type="post" data-id="2974">renovation work</a>.</li>



<li>Withdraw a taxable amount as income using Flexi Access Drawdown.  This did not use all the crystallised amount, so I have a crystallised balance still invested to drawdown from in future years.</li>



<li>Top up both household ISAs on top of the bed &amp; ISA transfer when the SIPP money hits the current account.</li>



<li>Fill out a P55 claim form because <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/practical-methods-for-draining-the-sipp/" data-type="post" data-id="2682">the taxable amount withdrawn is subject to month 1 payroll tax</a> which results in large over payment of tax.  This gets processed and returned to me in eight weeks.</li>



<li>Update my financial records with the capital gains recorded for the bed &amp; ISA and the SIPP withdrawal tracker.</li>
</ul>



<p>So quite a lot going on this year with one thing and another.  Next year should be a lot simpler because no tax free cash to withdraw and no bed &amp; ISA.</p>



<p>This plan won&#8217;t be fully executed until the end of May when the P55 refund happens, but the rest of it should start happening over the next week or two.</p>
