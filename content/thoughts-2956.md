<p>Plenty has been written on how people use AI (or not), but I&#8217;ve seen very little on the attitudes and style adopted when expressing their position.  Ava has written <a href="https://blog.avas.space/ai-ego/">some brilliantly sharp observations</a> that could form the basis of a taxonomic classification of the various AI tribes out there today.  </p>



<p>For example, this in response to the group who say you&#8217;ll get left behind if you don&#8217;t participate:</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-style-plain is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow is-style-plain--1">
<p>But tell me, were you left behind when you started using Excel late? Was it bad you only learned office stuff when you needed it? Were you not able to catch up? </p>



<p>Chances are, it makes no difference and with some effort and a workshop or YouTube videos, you can use the tools equally well. In the case of LLMs, using it has never been easier. You can just use plain, natural language! No submenus, settings, buttons, search operators and the like to remember. </p>



<p>It‘s&nbsp;<em><strong>designed to be easy</strong></em>.</p>
</blockquote>



<p>We are currently sitting in a primordial soup of models, tools and applications when it comes to AI.  Expressing any opinion on it, however earnestly, is likely to be rendered utterly obsolete in a few weeks.</p>



<p>Reading Ava&#8217;s balanced analysis of the various narratives forced me to re-examine my own. While I&#8217;m firmly in the broad camp of &#8220;AI fanboy/next industrial revolution etc&#8221;, my daily opinions on individual threads have definitely shifted with prevailing winds.</p>



<p>I&#8217;ve had two AI conversations this week that illustrate how uncertain my own feelings are when confronting the ever expanding wall of AI capability and influence.</p>



<p>The first came from a local WhatsApp group where a few of us are trying to understand what the broad effects might be at the town level, and how and whether we address those today. There&#8217;s all kinds of paths we could take, from following global AI news, understanding county policy, deep diving into specific technologies and so on.</p>



<p>I bumped into one of the members yesterday, and said perhaps it&#8217;s really just enough that the group exists at this stage.  We meet, we share, we brainstorm.  When concrete, real world impacts emerge, at least there&#8217;s a group in the town that can be ready to assist the community in the future, if required (and given humanity&#8217;s track record in managing systemic societal shifts, assistance will almost certainly be required).</p>



<p>The second conversation was with a friend of mine while we were doing habitat management on a grassland meadow in the Cotswolds.  Ruth told me how her 20 year old granddaughter had declared that she would never use AI because of the environmental impact of chatbots.  Ruth&#8217;s question to me was how would I respond to this?</p>



<p>I spend an <a href="https://diary.uncountable.uk/">extraordinary amount of time</a> on nature recovery projects.  I strongly believe we need to make both large and small scale interventions to prevent climate and ecological collapse.  I also spend an extraordinary amount of time talking to AI agents, both for improving my understanding of the world, and also to deliver improved functionality for the 10 or so websites I manage.</p>



<p>So my response to Ruth was not really the &#8220;wait and see&#8221; approach I advocate for our community group.  Instead it was a lot more direct:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The average UK citizen consumes 70,000 watt hours of primary energy per day.  This includes not only your household consumption of electricity and heating (roughly 16,000), but also your share of transport (30,000), commercial, agriculture, government services and everything else that constitutes our lifestyle.  For comparison, the US is 200,000 watt hours/day  and Burundi is 800.<br><br>Simply participating in the physical world in a developed economy is environmentally devastating (partly why I believe large scale interventions are necessary to offset ecological decline).<br><br>The average internet query is around 0.4 watt hours.  A complex LLM query is between 2-3 watt hours (although this is expected to reduce to 1.7 in 2026).<br><br>If you watch tiktok for 30 minutes a day, you are consuming around 40 watt hours.  It barely registers in the grand scheme of things.<br><br>If you were using complex AI queries 500 times a day, you&#8217;re barely 2% of your daily energy consumption.  However, bear in mind that if you are doing 500 AI interactions a day, you&#8217;re probably not taking a bus into town, having a meal out, watching a movie or any of the environmentally consequential alternate lifestyle decisions you could take instead.<br><br>Sitting at home all day on AI is probably better for the environment than whatever other shizzle you had planned.  <br><br>I simply don&#8217;t buy the argument that we shouldn&#8217;t adopt AI because it consumes energy.  The things we invented in the past consume a lot more.  And who knows &#8230; by using AI, it may help us make significant inroads into reducing the impact of all those other things we seemingly can&#8217;t live without. <br></li>



<li>The second part of my response to Ruth was much more blunt.  The gender divide on usage of LLMs is already significant, just three years after it&#8217;s introduction.  Women are being harmed by applications such as pornographic image manipulation.  Clerical and administrative jobs are likely to be severely reduced by AI in the next few years and these are dis-proportionally occupied by women.  Women whose jobs are displaced by AI take longer on average to find new work than men.  Frontier AI development is two thirds male.  And while overall chatbot users are roughly 50:50, men make more queries for longer periods, and therefore dominate the overall volume of compute available.<br><br>So a decision by a young educated woman not to participate in AI not only introduces potential personal friction while navigating the remainder of this decade and the next, but also does nothing to progress the cause of global inequality.<br><br>It may well be that men address the gender gap on their own, but I&#8217;m afraid we don&#8217;t have a terribly good track record of doing that.  Participation by women is the only way forward in my view.   </li>
</ul>



<p>Returning to Ava&#8217;s article, she also touched upon the opinions people hold because of the circle they move within:</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-style-plain is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow is-style-plain--2">
<p><em><strong>All your friends hate AI?</strong></em>&nbsp;Better not touch it, out of fear of social repercussions. They can’t talk with you about the bullshit it did last time they tried it, or ethical, privacy, or environmental concerns, because they just never actually cared to develop an opinion aside from not wanting to be hated by their circle. </p>



<p>That’s boring and people pleaser behavior. I think you look silly if you have no deeper reason to not use something, no interesting arguments.</p>
</blockquote>



<p>The impact of AI on humanity is a complicated topic, and to even understand a small fraction, you have to make considerable personal investment of energy and focus.  </p>



<p>We have not yet developed the norms, education, regulations or institutions to help society as a whole navigate the impact.  Until then, we&#8217;re on our own as individuals.</p>



<p>It&#8217;s both thrilling and terrifying to be alive to witness the birth of this technology. Like it or not, we&#8217;re about to experience a cambrian explosion of technology that will alter the trajectory for <a href="https://thoughts.uncountable.uk/we-are-an-ancient-civilisation/" data-type="post" data-id="1829">the remainder of our species existence</a>.</p>



<p>Best hop aboard.</p>
