<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I run a website for my local town called <a href="https://discoverdursley.org.uk/">Discover Dursley</a>. One of the platform sections is the local What&#8217;s On, and I&#8217;m always on the hunt for local events to add into the weekly roundup.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">So I follow hundred&#8217;s of local facebook pages which produces a handy stream I can quickly scan for event posters.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Of course, over the last year or so, the rise of the AI poster has been relentless.  You know the ones I mean.  They&#8217;re kinda hard to define, but a bit like life itself, you know it when you see it.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">I get why people do it.  A few years ago saw the rise of the Canva poster where the same handful of templates appeared over and over in different colours, fonts and trimmings.  Organising events is hard enough, without spending another three hours making a poster from scratch.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But I don&#8217;t think any individual AI poster particularly stands out in the sea of other posters.  They are all bold, packed with detail and calls to action. They try hard to activate senses and capture attention fleetingly.  But I think our brains are starting to filter them out.  They just become one more thing to scroll.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Every now and then I come across a poster which is definitely not AI.  Usually it has unreadable text on a photo or some other inaccessible feature that marks out the amateur poster maker.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It&#8217;s entirely possible this could be the way forward for engagement.  The messy human touch unmistakable, and causing the human reader to stop scrolling momentarily to connect with the message.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Not everything needs to be a prompt.</p>
